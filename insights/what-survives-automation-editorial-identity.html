What survives automation? Editorial identity.

By Averris Team January 12, 2025 5 min read

What survives automation? Editorial identity.

As AI handles more content tasks, a publisher’s unique editorial voice becomes their most valuable differentiator.

The Automation Wave

AI can now:

  • Write articles in seconds
  • Generate headlines that maximize clicks
  • Summarize complex topics
  • Translate between languages
  • Personalize content for individual readers
  • Optimize publishing schedules for engagement

For publishers, this creates both opportunities and existential questions: If AI can do the work, what’s left for human publishers to contribute?

What AI Can’t Replicate

While AI excels at certain tasks, it struggles with what makes great publishers truly valuable:

1. Editorial Judgment

AI can surface trending topics, but it can’t decide which stories matter—which ones will be relevant in five years, which ones challenge power, which ones give voice to the voiceless.

Human editors bring:

  • Moral judgment about what deserves attention
  • Long-term thinking beyond immediate clicks
  • Cultural intuition about what resonates and why
  • Courage to cover unpopular but important topics

2. Institutional Trust

People don’t just read content—they read The New York Times, The Economist, Le Monde. These brands represent:

  • Consistent editorial standards
  • Track records of accuracy
  • Accountability when mistakes happen
  • Values that readers identify with

AI can generate content, but it can’t build institutional trust that takes decades to establish.

3. Unique Perspective

Every great publisher has a distinct worldview:

  • The Atlantic: Intellectual, long-form, big-picture thinking
  • The Guardian: Progressive, investigative, UK-focused with global reach
  • Bloomberg: Financial, data-driven, business-oriented
  • Vox: Explainer-focused, accessible, millennial-friendly

This editorial identity shapes every decision: which stories to cover, what angle to take, what sources to prioritize, what tone to strike.

AI can mimic style, but it can’t authentically embody a coherent worldview developed over years.

4. Original Reporting

AI can summarize and synthesize existing information, but it can’t:

  • Cultivate sources over months or years
  • Conduct in-person interviews
  • Gain access to restricted events
  • Break news through investigative work
  • Provide firsthand accounts

Original reporting remains a fundamentally human endeavor.

5. Community and Relationship

Publishers aren’t just content factories—they’re community hubs:

  • Comment sections where readers engage
  • Events that bring people together
  • Newsletters that feel personal
  • Journalists who become trusted voices

These relationships are built on human connection that AI cannot replicate.

The Path Forward: Double Down on Identity

As automation handles routine tasks, publishers should invest more heavily in what makes them unique:

Define Your Editorial Identity

Be explicit about:

  • What you stand for
  • Who you serve
  • What lens you bring to stories
  • What you won’t compromise on

Make Editorial Judgment Visible

Show readers the why behind your decisions:

  • Why you covered this story
  • Why you used these sources
  • Why this angle matters
  • Why you made this editorial choice

Transparency builds trust and reinforces identity.

Invest in Original Work

Use AI to handle commodity content, but dedicate human resources to:

  • Investigative journalism
  • Original analysis and commentary
  • First-person reporting
  • Unique multimedia experiences

Cultivate Voice

Whether it’s individual journalists or institutional tone, voice matters:

  • Develop writers with distinctive perspectives
  • Maintain consistency across content
  • Let personality shine through
  • Don’t let AI blandness creep in

Build Community

Create spaces for readers to:

  • Engage with each other
  • Interact with journalists
  • Participate in events
  • Feel part of something larger than content consumption

The Risk of Losing Identity

Publishers who don’t invest in editorial identity risk becoming:

  • Interchangeable: If AI handles most content, why choose one publisher over another?
  • Irrelevant: Without original work, you’re just repackaging what’s already available
  • Untrustworthy: Without consistent editorial standards, readers won’t know what to expect
  • Unmemorable: Generic content doesn’t build loyal audiences

Examples in Action

The Information

This subscription-based tech publication built its entire model on:

  • High-touch original reporting
  • Clear editorial voice (skeptical, analytical)
  • Community features (events, networking)
  • Accountability (corrections are prominently displayed)

AI could never replicate what makes The Information valuable because it’s not about content volume—it’s about editorial judgment and community.

The Economist

Despite pressure to publish more frequently, The Economist maintains:

  • A distinctive voice (formal, British, analytical)
  • Strong editorial viewpoint (classical liberalism)
  • Selective coverage (only stories that matter)
  • Anonymous bylines (institutional voice over individual personalities)

This strong identity keeps readers loyal even as other sources provide faster news.

Conclusion

Automation will handle more of the mechanical aspects of publishing—writing summaries, translating content, personalizing delivery. But the core value of great publishers has never been mechanical.

What survives automation is what’s always mattered most: judgment, trust, perspective, originality, and community.

The publishers who thrive in the age of AI won’t be those who compete with automation—they’ll be those who double down on being irreplaceably human.

Your editorial identity isn’t just a nice-to-have. It’s the only thing AI can’t copy.
